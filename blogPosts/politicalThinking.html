Political Philosophy: A very short introduction by David Miller ☰

This is a fairly short book, it’s seven chapters and about 130 pages. In that, however, it contains a great wealth of information and is densely packed with unbiased insights into key questions that form the heart of political philosophy. In my opinion, it fulfils the purpose it sets out: give a very short introduction to the world of political philosophy. It argues from both ends of the basic political spectrum and gives each ideology’s argument the respect it deserves when being discussed. For someone who is just starting to get into politics and is looking for guidance on how each ideology differs as well as the key questions that each must strive to answer, I strongly recommend this book.

A feature of this book that I especially liked was that although the chapters were linked, it wasn’t integral to have read the previous chapter to read the next one; you could pick it up at any point. This makes it easy to read over a few weeks or even months if you are hard pressed for time. Even though I read it in one sitting, I know there are many people who don’t have that privilege and it’s one of the reasons they may be deterred from reading, but this book doesn’t pose that problem.

The integral part of the book is really the content, however, and although I touched upon it slightly in the introduction, I have yet to go into detail and provide a fuller insight into the book. I’ll begin with saying that the author does indeed provide a very short introduction that introduces readers to key aspects of political philosophy without being dragged down by technical terminology or losing a sense of the depth of the issues being discussed. It discusses why we need political philosophy, key arguments for and against political authority, the benefits of democracy and its downfalls - and how to overcome these. It then discusses freedom and how to define inviolable freedom versus that which can be sacrificed by the state for the good of wider society, providing arguments from both libertarian and authoritarian points of view, with the reader being left to draw their own conclusions on where the line between personal freedom and societal freedom is. The book then goes on to discuss justice, and its evolution between different circumstances, shedding light on how there is no theory that can be defined as perfect.

The questions asked in earlier chapters are then given further dimension by the last two chapters; the first concerned with how modern society may tackle these questions, with the rise in feminist movements and the multicultural nations that we live in. The second discusses justice on a larger scale: a global scale. It presents the arguments from cosmopolitanism camp of thought who believe there should only be one overarching government or that all states enter a permanent agreement to work together and uphold the principles of justice globally. The author provides arguments against these views too, but not in a disproportionate manner; giving the reader the capacity to draw their own conclusions.

The one thing that I did find to hinder my ability to enjoy this book was the overly complex sentence structures; and I have been guilty of this myself in my writing. Although it was infrequent, I found myself losing track of the wider sense of the sentence. This wasn’t due to the use of obscure terminology, as I mentioned earlier the terminology was fairly accessible, but rather the use of sentences that spanned multiple lines using many connectives (although I see the irony in the fact that this is a multi-line sentence). My advice to readers would be to ensure you are in a miasma without distractions, allowing you to direct you focus to the book.

Overall, I once again circle back to starting point: the book provides a clear and reliable introduction to political philosophy. As quoted on the back of the book, it is “Clearly written, widely accessible, comprehensive, and a reliable guide to important controversies…” (Lord Parekh).
